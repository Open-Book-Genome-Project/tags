## Pending

### Alternate History
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Thema FHAF (Alternative history); BISAC "Science Fiction / Alternative History"; major Goodreads shelf
- **Definition:** Works structured around a divergence from documented history — a pivotal moment that "went differently" — and the world that results from that change.
- **Distinguishing rule:** The counterfactual premise is structural; it defines what kind of world the story takes place in. Differs from `Historical` (which accepts documented history as given) and `Sci-Fi` (which extrapolates forward from the present). A story set in a world where the Confederacy won the Civil War is Alternate History regardless of whether it feels like Sci-Fi, Drama, or Thriller.
- **Parent genres:** Historical, Sci-Fi, Fantasy
- **Example works:** *The Man in the High Castle* (Dick), *SS-GB* (Deighton), *Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell* (Clarke — borderline)
- **Notes:** This is a structural framework, not a setting mashup. It clearly passes the No-Mashup Rule. Thema treats it as a subgenre of war/historical fiction. Strong candidate.

### Magical Realism
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Thema FYH (Magical realism); recognized literary critical category
- **Definition:** Works set in an otherwise realistic world where magical or supernatural events occur and are accepted as mundane by the characters — without the awe, fear, or systematic rules of Fantasy or Horror.
- **Distinguishing rule:** In Fantasy, the magic is wondrous and rules-bound; characters notice it. In Magical Realism, the magic is unremarkable; characters treat it as ordinary. In Horror, the supernatural is threatening. Magical Realism uses magic as a narrative and metaphorical tool in a realist register.
- **Parent genres:** Literary, Drama
- **Example works:** *One Hundred Years of Solitude* (García Márquez), *Beloved* (Morrison), *The House of the Spirits* (Allende), *Midnight's Children* (Rushdie)
- **Notes:** This is a well-established literary critical category with a distinct reader community. The definition is tight enough to distinguish it from Fantasy. Strong candidate. Note that some works labeled Magical Realism by critics are better tagged Literary + Fantasy — the key test is whether the magic is *unremarked upon* by characters.

### Noir
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Thema FHD (Crime & mystery: hard-boiled fiction); recognized critical/market category
- **Definition:** Works in a dark, fatalistic mode where moral ambiguity, urban corruption, and the psychological weight of a flawed world are structural features — not just atmosphere.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Differs from `Gothic` (which uses atmospheric decay in a more romantic/supernatural register) and `Psychological` (which focuses on interior mental states). Noir is defined by its moral universe: no one is innocent, institutions are corrupt, and the protagonist may lose even when they win.
- **Parent genres:** Crime, Mystery, Drama
- **Example works:** *The Maltese Falcon* (Hammett), *Chinatown* (film — reference), *James Ellroy's LA Quartet*, *Devil in a Blue Dress* (Mosley)
- **Notes:** "Hard-boiled" is a related but slightly narrower term (specifically American, specifically detective fiction). Noir is the broader structural/tonal category. Consider whether to use "Noir" or "Hard-Boiled" — recommend "Noir" for broader applicability.

### Cozy
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Major Goodreads/Amazon market category ("Cozy Mystery", "Cozy Fantasy"); emerging critical recognition
- **Definition:** Works in which potentially dark or violent subject matter (crime, magic, conflict) is treated with a light, warm, community-centered tone — emphasizing safety, charm, and resolution over tension and threat.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Differs from the mood `Cozy` (which describes how any book feels) in that Cozy as a subgenre is a structural choice about how conflict is handled. A Cozy Mystery still has a murder, but the body count stays off-page, the community is warm, and the resolution is satisfying. The mood and the subgenre often co-occur but are independent.
- **Parent genres:** Mystery, Fantasy, Romance
- **Example works:** *The No. 1 Ladies' Detective Agency* (McCall Smith), *Hamish Macbeth* series (Beaton), *A Psalm for the Wild-Built* (Becky Chambers)
- **Notes:** This is one of the fastest-growing market categories in publishing. The structural definition (light treatment of dark material, community-centered, safe resolution) is distinct from mood alone. Recommend accepting with a note in the README clarifying the mood vs. subgenre distinction.

### Solarpunk
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Emerging speculative fiction category; contrasted with Cyberpunk/Dystopian
- **Definition:** Works set in optimistic futures structured around ecological sustainability, community cooperation, and radical hope — the structural inverse of Dystopian.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Where Cyberpunk is high-tech/low-life and Dystopian shows societal collapse, Solarpunk shows a world that has solved (or is working to solve) ecological and social problems through technology and collective action. Not merely Utopian (which can be satirical or critical) — Solarpunk is earnestly constructive.
- **Parent genres:** Sci-Fi, Fantasy
- **Example works:** *A Psalm for the Wild-Built* (Chambers), *The Ministry for the Future* (Robinson — borderline), *A Ps for the Wild-Built* (Chambers)
- **Notes:** Emerging category with a growing and dedicated readership. Stylistically distinct from existing subgenres. Some overlap with Utopian, but Solarpunk is specifically ecological and community-focused rather than a thought-experiment about ideal societies.

## Rejected

### "Historical Fantasy"
- **Proposed:** (common usage)
- **Decision:** Rejected — 2026-04
- **Reasoning:** Violates the No-Mashup Rule. "Historical Fantasy" is a combination of two genres (`Historical` + `Fantasy`), not a structural framework. Works that are both Historical and Fantasy should be tagged with both genres. Creating a subgenre for every genre combination would produce combinatorial explosion.

### "Military Sci-Fi"
- **Proposed:** (common usage, Thema-adjacent)
- **Decision:** Rejected as subgenre — 2026-04
- **Reasoning:** This is a topic+genre combination, not a structural framework. Works that are `Sci-Fi` and deal with `main_topics:Military` or `main_topics:War` are adequately expressed with existing tags. The structural mechanics (space opera scale, hard sci-fi tech, character-driven drama) vary too much within "Military Sci-Fi" for it to be a coherent subgenre. Revisit if `War / Military` is accepted as a genre.

## Deferred

### "Weird Fiction / New Weird"
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Critical category; China Miéville, Jeff VanderMeer
- **Decision:** Deferred — 2026-04
- **Reasoning:** The definition is contested even among fans. Could be a subgenre of Horror, Literary, or Sci-Fi. Needs a definition precise enough to apply consistently. Revisit when the literary_movement type is established — "New Weird" may be better placed there.
