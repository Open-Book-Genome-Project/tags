# literary_tropes

**Definition:** Recognizable narrative devices, structural conventions, or character archetypes that recur across works — the "how" of storytelling at the level of familiar patterns.

---

## What is a Literary Trope?

A trope is a **recognizable convention** — a structural move, a character type, a plot device — that readers and writers have encountered enough times to name and expect. Tropes are neither good nor bad; they are the shared vocabulary of storytelling.

Tropes operate at the level of **pattern**. They are the recurring building blocks from which stories are assembled.

Think of tropes as the answer to: **"What familiar storytelling moves does this work use?"**

---

## Core Rules

**Do use literary_tropes to capture:**
- Named character archetypes (The Chosen One, The Wicked Stepmother, The Unreliable Narrator)
- Recognizable plot structures or devices (Love Triangle, Slow Burn, Enemies to Lovers)
- Narrative conventions that readers can name and anticipate (The Tragic Hero, The MacGuffin, The Twist Ending)

**Do NOT put in literary_tropes:**
- Abstract philosophical meaning (that's `literary_themes`)
- Broad genre or narrative mode (that's `genres`)
- Specific characters from a work (that's `people`)
- Physical objects in the story (that's `things`)

**The Recognition Test:** If a well-read person would immediately recognize the term as a named storytelling convention — and might roll their eyes or delight at seeing it — it is a trope.

**The Pattern Rule:** Tropes are patterns across *many* works. If it's specific to one work, it's not a trope.

---

## Tropes vs. Themes vs. Subgenres

| | Trope | Theme | Subgenre |
|---|---|---|---|
| What it describes | A recognizable narrative pattern | An abstract meaning the story explores | The structural logic/framework of the plot |
| Level | Convention | Philosophy | Architecture |
| Example | Love Triangle | Love | Gothic |
| Question | "What familiar moves does it use?" | "What is it really about?" | "How is it structured?" |

A story can have the **subgenre** of Psychological, explore the **theme** of Identity, and use the **tropes** of Unreliable Narrator and Twist Ending — all simultaneously.

---

## Character Archetypes vs. Narrative Devices

Tropes include both:

**Character archetypes:** recurring character types across works
- The Chosen One, The Mentor, The Trickster, The Antihero, The Femme Fatale

**Narrative devices:** recurring plot or structural conventions
- Love Triangle, Found Family, Slow Burn, The MacGuffin, Enemies to Lovers, The Twist
