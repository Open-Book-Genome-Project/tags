## Pending

### The Prophecy
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Definition:** A foretold future — delivered by oracle, prophecy, or fate — that drives or shapes the protagonist's actions and choices, regardless of whether it ultimately proves true or self-fulfilling.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Differs from `Chosen One` (which is about the protagonist's unique destiny) in that a Prophecy can apply to any character and may be wrong, misinterpreted, or circumvented. A Chosen One story often involves a Prophecy, but a Prophecy story does not require a Chosen One (e.g., prophecies that doom minor characters, structural prophecies in tragedy).
- **Example works:** *Macbeth* (Shakespeare), *Harry Potter* (Rowling), *The Wheel of Time* (Jordan), *Oedipus Rex* (Sophocles)
- **Notes:** Strong candidate. The Prophecy is a recognizable narrative device with a long literary history, distinct from Chosen One, and commonly used in Fantasy, Mythology, and Tragedy.

### Fish Out of Water
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Definition:** A protagonist placed in an environment fundamentally foreign to their background, personality, or expectations — generating comedy, drama, or growth from the mismatch.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Differs from `Coming of Age` (subgenre, which is about the transition to adulthood) in that Fish Out of Water can happen at any life stage and is about *situational mismatch*, not developmental transition. The protagonist doesn't need to change; the comedy or drama can come entirely from the situation.
- **Example works:** *The Princess Diaries* (Cabot), *A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court* (Twain), *An American in Paris*, *Fish* (Walters)
- **Notes:** Strong candidate. Immediately recognizable, applicable across all genres, and produces a distinct narrative mode.

### Dark Night of the Soul
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Screenwriting/narrative theory term (Christopher Vogler, Save the Cat); originally from St. John of the Cross
- **Definition:** The structural nadir of a protagonist's journey — the moment of maximum doubt, despair, or apparent failure before the final act's resolution or defeat.
- **Distinguishing rule:** This is a structural marker (it occurs at a specific point in the narrative arc), not a mood or theme. Differs from `Harrowing` (mood, describes the reading experience) and `Grief` (theme, an ongoing subject). The Dark Night of the Soul is a *moment*, typically followed by a choice or revelation.
- **Example works:** Most traditional three-act narratives; *The Return of the King* (Tolkien), *Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows* (Rowling), *Cast Away* (film reference)
- **Notes:** Moderate candidate. The term is widely used in narrative theory. Concern: it's so broadly applicable (most stories have one) that it may not add meaningful signal. Stronger as a tag for works where the dark night is unusually prolonged or is itself the primary subject.

### Chekhov's Gun
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Definition:** An element introduced early in a narrative that later becomes significant to the plot — the principle that nothing in a well-crafted story is superfluous.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Chekhov's Gun is a craft technique, not a subject or character type. It describes the *structural economy* of the narrative. Differs from `MacGuffin` (which is an object whose content is irrelevant) in that Chekhov's Gun is significant precisely because of what it is.
- **Example works:** *Crime and Punishment* (Dostoevsky), essentially any Agatha Christie novel, *Of Mice and Men* (Steinbeck)
- **Notes:** Uncertain candidate. The trope is recognizable to literate readers, but it's a craft *principle* that applies to most well-crafted fiction rather than a distinguishing feature of specific works. May be too universal to add meaningful signal. Recommend deferring until a better definition can distinguish "uses Chekhov's Gun consciously" from "just good plotting."

### Red Herring
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Definition:** A deliberately misleading clue, character, or plot element introduced to misdirect the reader's suspicion or expectations.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Primarily relevant for Mystery and Thriller. Differs from `The Twist` (which is a major revelation) in that Red Herrings are intentional misdirections that *don't* reveal new truth — they divert attention from the real truth.
- **Example works:** Any Agatha Christie novel, *Gone Girl* (Flynn), *And Then There Were None* (Christie)
- **Notes:** Moderate candidate. Very specific to Mystery/Thriller, which raises the question of whether it passes the cross-genre test for tropes. Tropes should ideally apply across genres. However, the term is so widely used that it may be worth including with a note about its primary domain.

### Reluctant Hero
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Definition:** A protagonist who actively resists taking on the hero's role — through fear, selfishness, incompetence, or genuine preference for an ordinary life — before being compelled by circumstance.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Differs from `Antihero` (who *is* the protagonist but lacks heroic qualities) in that the Reluctant Hero typically *has* the qualities needed but doesn't want to use them. The Antihero is the hero the story deserves; the Reluctant Hero is the one who didn't sign up for this.
- **Example works:** *The Hobbit* (Tolkien), *Harry Potter* (Rowling — Neville Longbottom), *Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy* (Adams)
- **Notes:** Strong candidate. Widely recognized, distinct from Antihero, applicable across Fantasy, Comedy, Adventure, and more.

## Rejected

### "Deus Ex Machina"
- **Proposed:** 2026-04
- **Decision:** Rejected — 2026-04
- **Reasoning:** Deus Ex Machina describes a *flaw* in plotting (a convenient, unearned resolution) rather than an intentional narrative device. Tagging works with it would be a critical judgment, not a classification. The taxonomy should describe works, not evaluate their craft quality. An author who uses a sudden resolution intentionally (irony, parody) is doing something different from one who uses it because they wrote themselves into a corner.

## Deferred

### "Chosen One's Refusal" / "Hero's Journey"
- **Proposed:** 2026-04
- **Decision:** Deferred — 2026-04
- **Reasoning:** The Hero's Journey (Campbell's monomyth) is so broadly applicable that it may not add meaningful signal. The Chosen One's Refusal is a specific *beat* in that journey. Both need tighter definitions that distinguish "deliberately uses the Hero's Journey structure" from "is a story with a protagonist" before they can be tagged consistently. Revisit with specific examples that the tag would *exclude*.
