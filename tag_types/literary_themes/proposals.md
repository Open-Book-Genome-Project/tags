# literary_themes — Proposals

Tracks proposed additions to the `literary_themes` open vocabulary.
Because this is an open/growing type, the bar for acceptance is lower than for controlled types — but the definition must still be precise and distinct.
See [AGENTS.md](../AGENTS.md) for evaluation criteria.

---

## Pending

### Alienation
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Core existentialist and modernist literary concept; present in Camus, Kafka, Sartre
- **Definition:** The experience of estrangement — from society, from other people, or from one's own self — as a defining condition of modern existence.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Differs from `Isolation` (which is physical separation) and `Rejection` (which involves active exclusion by others). Alienation is an *existential* condition — the character may be surrounded by people and still be fundamentally alienated. It is often a chosen or structural state, not a response to a single event.
- **Example works:** *The Stranger* (Camus), *The Trial* (Kafka), *Notes from Underground* (Dostoevsky), *The Catcher in the Rye* (Salinger)
- **Notes:** Strong candidate. One of the central concepts of 20th-century literature with a clear, established critical meaning.

### Belonging
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Definition:** The search for community, acceptance, and a place where one is recognized and valued; the experience of finding or failing to find one's people.
- **Distinguishing rule:** The inverse of `Alienation` and the complement of `Identity`. Where Alienation describes estrangement, Belonging describes the desire for connection. Can be treated as a theme even when the character fails to find belonging — the *search* is the theme.
- **Example works:** *The Perks of Being a Wallflower* (Chbosky), *Giovanni's Room* (Baldwin), *Middlesex* (Eugenides)
- **Notes:** Strong candidate. Particularly relevant for LGBTQ+ and immigrant narratives, coming-of-age stories, and family sagas.

---

### Exile
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Definition:** The condition of displacement — from one's homeland, culture, language, or community — and the longing, loss, and transformation that displacement produces.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Differs from `Alienation` (which is existential/psychological) in that Exile is specifically about *place and belonging to a place*. Can be physical (a refugee) or cultural (a second-generation immigrant). The homeland may be real, remembered, or mythologized.
- **Example works:** *The Namesake* (Lahiri), *Americanah* (Adichie), *The Kite Runner* (Hosseini), *Speak, Memory* (Nabokov)
- **Notes:** Strong candidate. Exile as a theme spans postcolonial literature, immigrant narratives, and refugee stories — a growing and important body of work.

---

### Forgiveness
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Definition:** The moral and emotional process of releasing resentment, blame, or the desire for retribution — whether toward another or toward oneself — and what it costs or enables.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Differs from `Redemption` (which is about the wrongdoer's journey to become better) in that Forgiveness is about the *wronged party's* journey. Redemption is earned; Forgiveness is granted (or withheld). A story about a person deciding to forgive is a Forgiveness story. A story about a person making amends is a Redemption story.
- **Example works:** *The Kite Runner* (Hosseini), *Peace Like a River* (Enger), *The Shack* (Young)
- **Notes:** Moderate candidate. The distinction from Redemption is real but subtle. Worth accepting given that it captures a genuine, distinct moral journey.

---

### Shame
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Psychological and philosophical distinction (Brené Brown, Sartre, Nussbaum)
- **Definition:** The experience of feeling fundamentally flawed, unworthy, or exposed as inadequate in the eyes of others (or one's internalized audience) — distinct from guilt in that shame is about *being*, not *doing*.
- **Distinguishing rule:** `Guilt` = "I did something bad." `Shame` = "I am something bad." Guilt motivates repair; Shame motivates hiding, collapse, or aggression. This distinction, well-established in moral psychology, maps onto meaningfully different narrative arcs.
- **Example works:** *A Thousand Splendid Suns* (Hosseini), *The Scarlet Letter* (Hawthorne), *Giovanni's Room* (Baldwin), *Hanya Yanagihara's work*
- **Notes:** Strong candidate. The Guilt/Shame distinction is well-grounded in psychology and produces different narrative arcs. Worth the slot.

---

### Humanity / What It Means to Be Human
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Definition:** The interrogation of what distinguishes humans from non-humans (animals, machines, aliens, gods) — or what is worth preserving, aspiring to, or lamenting about human nature.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Particularly relevant for Sci-Fi (AI, robots, aliens), Fantasy (elves, gods), and Literary fiction (absurdism, existentialism). Differs from `Identity` (which is about individual selfhood) in that Humanity is about the *species* or *category* of being.
- **Example works:** *Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?* (Dick), *Never Let Me Go* (Ishiguro), *Frankenstein* (Shelley), *The Left Hand of Darkness* (Le Guin)
- **Notes:** Strong candidate for Sci-Fi and speculative fiction, but also relevant beyond genre. The theme is common enough to warrant inclusion.

## Deferred

### "Progress"
- **Proposed:** 2026-04
- **Decision:** Deferred — 2026-04
- **Reasoning:** Progress as a theme is real (the question of whether human advancement improves or degrades life) but it overlaps heavily with `Technology`, `Power`, and `Justice` depending on context. The definition needs to be precise enough to distinguish it from those themes. Revisit when more examples can be gathered.

### "Nihilism"
- **Proposed:** 2026-04
- **Decision:** Deferred — 2026-04
- **Reasoning:** Nihilism is more of a philosophical position that a book might *express* or *critique* than a theme a book *wrestles with*. A book that takes nihilism as its premise (everything is meaningless) would be tagged with it, but so would a book that argues against nihilism — making the tag ambiguous about what it signals. Revisit with a tighter definition.
