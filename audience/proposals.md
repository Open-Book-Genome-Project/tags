## Pending

### New Adult
- **Proposed:** 2026-04 (initial)
- **Source:** Recognized publishing category since ~2009; major Amazon/Goodreads shelf
- **Definition:** Works featuring protagonists and themes centered on the transitional period between Young Adult and full adulthood — typically ages 18–25 — including college, early career, first serious relationships, and leaving home.
- **Distinguishing rule:** Differs from `Young Adult` (which centers the teenage/high school experience) in that New Adult is about the next phase: legal adulthood without full independence, first encounters with adult institutions (college, workplace, finances). Content may include explicit material that Young Adult typically avoids.
- **Example works:** *Easy* (Wallflower), *Beautiful Disaster* (McGuire), *Fangirl* (Rowell — borderline YA/NA)
- **Notes:** Moderate candidate. New Adult is a real publishing category with distinct content and reader demographics. The concern is that it's primarily a *content level* signal (more mature than YA) rather than an *audience* signal — it targets adults who want to read about that life stage, not people currently in that life stage. Needs discussion before accepting.

## Rejected

### "Reluctant Readers"
- **Proposed:** (common library term)
- **Decision:** Rejected — 2026-04
- **Reasoning:** "Reluctant reader" describes a reader characteristic, not an audience the author designed for. It is a library programming term used to select accessible books, not a meaningful work-level classification. Books selected for reluctant readers span many formats, genres, and ages. This information belongs in library collection notes, not the work taxonomy.

### "ESL / English Language Learners"
- **Proposed:** (common library term)
- **Decision:** Rejected as audience — 2026-04
- **Reasoning:** Language level is separate from audience age/intent. A book "for ESL learners" is defined by its linguistic accessibility, not its subject audience. This is better handled as a `content_feature` (simplified language, bilingual text) or as metadata in the edition record. May revisit if a `reading_level` type is established.
