# moods

**Definition:** The emotional resonance or "vibe" felt by the reader *during or after* the reading experience.

---

## What is a Mood?

A mood tag describes the **reader's felt experience** — what it is like to be inside this book. It is not the book's subject, its plot, or its genre. It is the emotional weather.

Moods are distinct from genres in a crucial way: a Horror novel can be **Cozy** (cozy horror is a real subculture), a Tragedy can be **Uplifting**, a Romance can be **Harrowing**. Moods cross genre boundaries freely.

---

## Core Rules

**Do use moods to capture:**
- The dominant emotional tone a reader would use to describe the experience of reading the book
- The atmosphere or "feel" of the prose, pacing, and world
- What a reader might say in a review when describing how the book made them feel

**Do NOT put in moods:**
- The book's subject or topic (that's `main_topics`)
- The genre or narrative mode (that's `genres`)
- The author's intent — focus on the reader's experience

**The Reader Perspective Rule:** Ask "how does this book *feel* to read?" not "what is this book *about*?"

**The Cross-Genre Rule:** If a mood can only apply to one genre, it probably isn't a mood — it's a genre characteristic. Good moods work across Horror, Romance, Sci-Fi, and Literary alike.

**The Distinctness Rule:** Moods should not be synonyms of existing moods. Before adding a new term, check that it describes a meaningfully different emotional register.

---

## Relationship to Other Types

Moods complement genres and subgenres but do not replace them:

| Genre | Example Moods |
|---|---|
| Horror | Foreboding, Disturbing, Harrowing, Intense |
| Romance | Swoonworthy, Cozy, Melancholic, Happy |
| Thriller | Suspenseful, Thrilling, Intense, Overwhelming |
| Literary | Melancholic, Nostalgic, Humbling, Inspiring |
| Comedy | Playful, Comical, Happy, Whimsical |

A single work can carry multiple moods. A Gothic Romance might be Atmospheric, Melancholic, and Swoonworthy simultaneously.

---

## Proposing a New Mood

See [CONTRIBUTING.md](../CONTRIBUTING.md). A new mood must:

1. Describe a distinct emotional experience felt by the reader
2. Be applicable across multiple genres (not locked to one)
3. Not be a synonym or near-duplicate of an existing mood term
4. Be a feeling, not a subject, setting, or structural device
