# genres

**Definition:** The stylistic "vibe" or market category/tradition the book belongs to. The emotional promise made to the reader.

---

## What is a Genre?

A genre defines the **primary mode of storytelling** and the **emotional experience** the reader expects. When a bookseller puts a book on a shelf, or a streaming service recommends "if you liked X," they are operating on genre.

Genres are broad. They are the first answer to "what kind of book is this?" — not the full answer.

---

## Core Rules

**Do use genres to capture:**
- The dominant emotional register the work aims to produce (Horror → fear, Comedy → amusement, Thriller → tension)
- The storytelling tradition or market category the work belongs to
- What a librarian or bookseller would call it on signage

**Do NOT put in genres:**
- Words that describe what the book is *about* — those are `main_topics` (e.g., "War", "Love", "Politics")
- Structural devices — those are `subgenres` (e.g., "Locked Room", "Epistolary")
- Physical form — those are `content_formats` (e.g., "Novel", "Graphic Novel")
- The words "Fiction" or "Nonfiction" — these are too broad to be useful as genres

**The Emotion Rule:** If the word describes the feeling the book seeks to evoke, it is a genre.

**The Standalone Rule:** A top-level genre must be a primary mode of storytelling capable of standing alone. "Psychological" is not a genre — it modifies a genre (it belongs in `subgenres`). "Mystery" is a genre.

**The Directional Rule (Mystery vs. Thriller):** Mystery looks *backward* (solving a past crime); Thriller looks *forward* (stopping an imminent threat).

---

## What is NOT a Genre

| Term | Why it's wrong here | Correct type |
|---|---|---|
| Historical Fiction | Contains "Fiction"; also "Historical" alone is sufficient | `subgenres` → Historical |
| Psychological | Modifies a genre, doesn't stand alone | `subgenres` → Psychological |
| Time Travel | A narrative device/prop | `things` or `main_topics` |
| War | A subject | `main_topics` |
| Classic | A reception/status descriptor, not a narrative mode | consider `literary_form` or drop |
| Novel | Physical form | `content_formats` |

---

## Relationship to Subgenres

A work can have one or more genres, and one or more subgenres. Subgenres narrow the genre:

- Genre: `Sci-Fi` + Subgenre: `Cyberpunk`
- Genre: `Mystery` + Subgenre: `Locked Room`
- Genre: `Horror` + Subgenre: `Gothic`

A genre should be able to stand meaningfully without a subgenre. A subgenre rarely makes sense without its parent genre context.

## Proposing a New Genre

See [CONTRIBUTING.md](../CONTRIBUTING.md). A new genre must:

1. Define a primary mode of storytelling or emotional promise
2. Not be expressible as a combination of existing genres
3. Have a significant body of works in Open Library
4. Be recognized by librarians or booksellers as a shelf category
