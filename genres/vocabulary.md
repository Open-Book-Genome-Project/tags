# Genres — Controlled Vocabulary

A managed list. To propose additions or changes, see [CONTRIBUTING.md](../CONTRIBUTING.md).

Tag format: `genre:term` (e.g., `genre:horror`)

---

| Tag | Definition |
|---|---|
| Absurd | Works in which reason and logic are systematically undermined; the universe is indifferent or irrational |
| Action | Plot-driven works where physical conflict, speed, and kinetic energy are the primary draw |
| Adventure | Works centered on a journey, quest, or exploration — often into unfamiliar or dangerous territory |
| Comedy | Works whose primary intent is to amuse; structured around comic situations, misunderstandings, or wit |
| Crime | Works in which criminal activity (planning, execution, or consequence) is the central subject |
| Drama | Works that explore serious human situations and emotional conflict without a dominant genre framework |
| Erotica | Works in which explicit sexual content is a primary and intentional element |
| Fantasy | Works set in worlds with magic, mythological creatures, or supernatural systems that operate by internal rules |
| Historical | Works set substantially in a documented past era, where that era's conditions shape the story |
| Horror | Works whose primary intent is to produce fear, dread, or revulsion in the reader |
| Humor | Works that use comedy as a stylistic mode rather than a structural one — lighter than Comedy |
| LGBTQ+ | Works in which queer identity, relationships, or experience is a central (not incidental) element |
| Literary | Works where language, form, and prose style are foregrounded as primary artistic concerns |
| Mystery | Works structured around the retrospective discovery of the truth about a hidden event (typically a crime) |
| Mythology | Works drawing substantially from or retelling mythological traditions |
| Romance | Works in which a central love relationship and its emotional development drives the plot |
| Satire | Works that use irony, exaggeration, or ridicule to critique society, institutions, or human nature |
| Sci-Fi | Works that extrapolate from scientific or technological possibility into speculative scenarios |
| Thriller | Works structured around tension, urgency, and the forward-looking prevention of catastrophe |
| Tragedy | Works in which the protagonist's downfall — through flaw, circumstance, or fate — is the central arc |
| Western | Works set in the American West (typically 19th century) and engaging its themes of frontier, law, and survival |
