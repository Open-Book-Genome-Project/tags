# main_topics

**Definition:** The primary concepts, academic subject matter, or central subjects that the work is substantially *about*. The overarching "point."

---

## What is a Main Topic?

A main topic answers: **"What is this book dealing with at its core?"** Not the emotional meaning (that's `literary_themes`), not the setting (that's `places`), not a character (that's `people`) — but the *subject matter*, the field of inquiry, the central concern.

Main topics are the concepts a librarian would use for subject cataloging: the Dewey or LC subject headings translated into plain, searchable language.

---

## Core Rules

**Do use main_topics to capture:**
- The primary academic, intellectual, or subject-matter focus (War, Medicine, Labor, Class, Magic Systems)
- Concepts the work directly and substantially addresses
- What a librarian would use for subject headings

**Do NOT put in main_topics:**
- The abstract philosophical meaning of the work (that's `literary_themes`)
- Physical objects present in the story (that's `things`)
- Settings or locations (that's `places`)
- Structural or narrative conventions (that's `literary_tropes`)
- Background elements or world-building details (that's `sub_topics`)

**The Centrality Test:** A main topic is not just *mentioned* — it is *central*. If removing this concept from the book would require rewriting the core premise, it's a main topic. If the book could exist without it, it might be a `sub_topic`.

**The Catalog Test:** Would a librarian use this as a primary subject heading? If yes, it's a main topic.

---

## Main Topics vs. Sub Topics

| | Main Topic | Sub Topic |
|---|---|---|
| What it describes | The central subject or focus | Supporting or background elements |
| Centrality | Drives the plot or argument | Populates the world without driving it |
| Example (Wuthering Heights) | Interpersonal relations, Class, Inheritance | Cousins, Country life, Manners and customs |

---

## This is an Open/Growing List

Main topics are not strictly controlled — the world of subjects is too large. Additions are made via PR with a brief note on what specific works prompted the addition.

When adding a topic:
- Use the most common, plain-English term (avoid jargon unless jargon is standard in the field)
- Prefer broad terms over overly specific ones (prefer `Medicine` over `19th-century surgical practices`)
- Check `sub_topics` to see if it belongs there instead
