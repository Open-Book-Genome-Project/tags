# literary_themes

**Definition:** The abstract, philosophical, or emotional drivers that animate a work — the "why" and "what does it mean" questions the book is wrestling with.

---

## What is a Literary Theme?

A theme is the **internal engine** of a work. It is the question the author is asking, the tension they're exploring, the human truth they're reaching toward.

Themes are abstract. They are not events, settings, characters, or objects. They are the *meaning* that the story's events generate.

Think of themes as the answer to: **"If I had to explain what this book is really about — beneath the plot — what would I say?"**

---

## Core Rules

**Do use literary_themes to capture:**
- Abstract emotional or philosophical drivers (Love, Revenge, Mortality, Identity, Power)
- The "Why" or internal logic behind a work's events
- Concepts the work actively interrogates, not just mentions

**Do NOT put in literary_themes:**
- Specific characters, places, or events (those belong in `people`, `places`, `things`)
- Plot devices or narrative structures (those belong in `subgenres` or `literary_tropes`)
- Broad genre descriptors (those belong in `genres`)
- Topics that are about the *external* subject matter rather than the *internal* meaning (those belong in `main_topics`)

**The Abstraction Test:** Can you replace the word with "the nature of ___"? If yes, it's likely a theme.
- "Love" → "the nature of love" ✓ theme
- "War" → "the nature of war" ✓ could be theme or topic — context matters
- "Yorkshire" → "the nature of Yorkshire" ✗ not a theme — it's a place

**The Interrogation Rule:** A theme is not just mentioned in a book — it is actively explored, questioned, or complicated. A book that features horses is not necessarily "about" animals. A book that questions what it means to be human through non-human characters has the theme of Identity or Humanity.

---

## Themes vs. Topics

| Distinction | Theme | Topic |
|---|---|---|
| Level of abstraction | High — philosophical/emotional | Medium — conceptual/academic |
| Example | Mortality, Betrayal, Redemption | Death rituals, War crimes, Medical ethics |
| Question it answers | "What is this book really about?" | "What does this book deal with?" |
| Test | "The nature of ___" | "Books about ___" |

A book about the death penalty deals with the **topic** of capital punishment and may explore the **theme** of Justice or State Power.

---

## This is an Open/Growing List

Unlike genres, moods, and content formats, literary themes are not strictly controlled. The vocabulary is open and grows as contributors tag works. Common themes are listed in `vocabulary.md` as starting points, not limits.

When adding a new theme:
1. Use the most general abstraction that still has meaning (prefer `Mortality` over `Fear of Death`)
2. Check that an existing theme doesn't already cover the concept
3. Use a noun or noun phrase, not a verb or sentence
